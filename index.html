ABOUT

Originally from Tulsa, Oklahoma, Jack attended Holland Hall from 2001 until 2017. (That's no typo, he went to the same school from Pre-K through the twelfth grade.) Jack then attended Villanova University, where he received his B.S. in Computer Science. While at Villanova, Jack was the president of the Association for Computing Machinery, the station manager at WXVU 89.1 FM, and the host of The Come Up every Friday night from 8:30-10 PM. At the height of the pandemic, he worked with the Office of the President to produce Think Negative!, a podcast that kept Villanovans on campus and around the world abreast of the university's response to the crisis.

As a junior at Villanova, Jack began working for threshold.world as an intern, becoming a full developer before his senior year and joining the company full-time after graduation. At threshold, he leads product development on b.world, a platform helping nonprofits and other social impact teams to manage projects, measure impact, and tell their stories.

In 2023, one of Jack's college roommates talked him into starting a podcast where they would analyze seasons of the reality-competition show Survivor, appropriately titled Survivor Analytics. Now in its sixth season, Survivor Analytics has 17,000 followers on TikTok and counting. Multiple former Survivor contestants have appeared on the show, including Survivor: China winner Todd Herzog.

Jack now lives in Washington D.C. with his incredible partner but without any dogs, because you can only be so lucky.

TECH

Jack has been developing software professionally for roughly a decade, a fact that gives him an existential crisis every time he remembers it.

Though he got his start in iOS development (back in the Swift 1.0 days), Jack's primary experience is in full-stack web development. Through high school and college, he built and published several experiemental platforms on his own while also working part-time as a web developer. He now works full time at threshold.world, where he leads development on their SaaS platform b.world. He is well-versed in .NET, PHP, Node, and plenty of other technologies.

b.world

b.world is a project management platform for impact-oriented teams. One of my favorite features is Insights, which gives users the power to capture qualitative data at any point in a project's life-cycle, then use those Insights to drive powerful storytelling.

Zettelkasten

A Node script and Azure OCR power this system that auto-parses my handwritten papers and notecards and files them into an Obsidian vault, with smart indexing and relationships.

Ipseity

Ipseity was my senior project at Villanova, a proof of concept for real-time customizable spatialized audio in teleconferencing. Watch a demo here.

dot

dot was my last published iOS app, a post-Vine, pre-TikTok platform for looping fullscreen vertical video. It was surprisingly expensive for a college kid making minimum wage to support storage for that much data, and as such, did not last very long.

tangle

tangle was a social network I built that allowed users to upload almost any medium of content, so long as it fit in a little rectangle.

gronolo

gronolo was my first experimental social app, a messenger where you could only send one of three emojis. Over 100k emojis were sent before I took it down.

Holland Hall Schedule App

My high school used a complex modular system on a six-day cycle, and this app helped students keep track of where they were in their days. I wrote this app in Swift 1.0, and the backend was a tiny server that let the school update for snow days and things like that.

WRITING

By volume of work, it could be argued that Jack is a baseball writer, but he largely focuses on the intersection of technology and humanity. Baseball's merely an answer to that great question. His first published work examined the ethical failings in Boeing's development of the 737 MAX MCAS system, and how their response resembled that of the Therac-25 disaster of the 1980s.

Jack began working on his master's in creative writing and literature from Harvard University in the spring of 2023, and plans to graduate in the spring of 2026. He is currently revising his first novel, The Whetstone, which will serve as the capstone of his degree.

In addition to that and other longer works, he regularly writes an irregular sports column on his Substack and occasionally publishes short fiction there.

"It Stirred Her Tremendously"

Reading Zora Neale Hurston's Their Eyes Were Watching God as a work of black feminist transcendentalism and intersectional liberation.

"In the society of Eyes, which is only a few degrees removed from our own, the power structure is built upon the exploitation of nature and of those most oppressed peoples (African-Americans and especially African American women), and that system of oppression is enforced by artificial and manufactured laws. Janie, a paragon of her caste, is deeply attuned to the inevitable realities of the physical world, and as such liberates herself by realizing that the man-made society can never overcome the laws of nature (including that final law, death). When she lives in accordance with nature, why would she be cowed by the flimsy principles of white-and-male society?"

Conrad's Romanticism

Joseph Conrad's Lord Jim can be easily read as a mere work of genre fiction, a rollicking oceanic adventure based on a true story. But dig a layer deeper, and it is a heartbreaking exploration of the perils that one can face when living life to the fullest.

"Any man willing to leave the comforts of Western civilization to pursue the tribulations of the open ocean is probably a romantic soul in one way or another. The question then becomes not one of ‘if’, but one of ‘how’. How do they live with their romanticism? How do they control it, or does it control them?"

A Goddess of Two Sorts

Examining the intentionally muddled parallels between Joyce's Ulysses and Homer's Odyssey in the first chapter of that great Irish work, specifically the role of the milkmaid as both Muse and Athena.

"The parodic nature of Ulysses creates ample opportunity for interpretation on countless levels. One of the crucial dimensions of Joyce’s work to watch closely is the distance between his work and the original. Though there are many parallels between the Odyssey and Ulysses, they are not geometrically parallel, running on identical paths without intersection. The deviations between the two are always intentional, and their implications are crucial to understanding at least a fraction of Joyce’s intentions."

Writ in Water

Published in The Coffee Ring Review Writ in Water explores the implications of constant social media consumption and augmented reality on self-image and identity.

"Controlling the mask was second nature to Diona. A combination of eye movements, minute tensions of eyebrows and lips, and murmured verbal commands quickly prepared her for the rave within. Silver glitter appeared on her cheeks, and with few actions the color shifted to match her blue top. A similar shade spread like wildfire over her lips. She thought, oh what the hell, and her eyes began to gleam blue as well. A bit of lightning perpetually circled her irises, the electric flickering amplified off the glitter like a disco ball. Diona smiled, satisfied with the rush job. As she worked, notifications she’d been ignoring began to scroll through the chyron under her right eye. Client requests, messages from friends confused about why she’d left the silent party, plenty of spam. Her smile disappeared and she dismissed the alerts with a frustrated blink. With one last check, she took a deep breath and left the restroom."
